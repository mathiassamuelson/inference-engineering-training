# Deploying Gemma 4 31B on Two RTX 3090s: What Actually Worked

@Google DeepMind released Gemma 4 31B Dense a few days ago. I've spent the last two days running it locally on a pair of @NVIDIA RTX 3090s (48GB total VRAM). Here's what I learned — including a gotcha about consumer multi-GPU builds that cost me 21% of my prefill throughput on a single configuration change.

---

**The setup**

Two RTX 3090s with an NVLink bridge. llama.cpp with the Q8_0 GGUF from ggml-org on @Hugging Face, loaded with layer splitting (`-ngl 999`, `CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES=0,2`). Roughly half the 60 layers live on each card, and activations flow between them during every forward pass.

The fit is tight: ~22.8GB used on each 24GB card after weights and KV cache. There is no headroom for anything else running on those GPUs.

The reason this fits at all is Gemma 4's hybrid attention architecture. Of the 60 layers, 50 use sliding-window attention (1024-token window, 16 KV heads) and only 10 use global full-context attention with just 4 KV heads. Since global layers are the most memory-expensive — they store the full context — cutting their head count is where the biggest savings come from. Without this design, a 31B model at Q8_0 with a 100K+ context wouldn't come close to fitting on 48GB.

---

**The day-1 bug**

llama.cpp added Gemma 4 support within hours of the model's release. That kind of turnaround is impressive — and also means bugs. I hit a hard segfault at approximately 5,500 prompt tokens, which was a blocker for my use case (a ~6K system prompt alone). I filed a repro on GitHub. The maintainers had it fixed overnight.

Shoutout to the llama.cpp team. That's an excellent response for an open-source project dealing with a brand-new model architecture on day one.

---

**Performance on NVLink**

After the fix, I ran a prompt-length sweep from 500 to 28,000 tokens:

```
Prompt Tokens   Prefill tok/s   Decode tok/s
    519              862           23.9
    954              844           23.6
  1,837              920           23.0
  3,585              947           22.1
  7,086            1,089           21.6
 14,085            1,173           21.3
 28,085            1,158           20.4
```

Decode is remarkably flat: a 14.6% drop across a 54x span in context length. That's about as good as it gets for a 31B dense model — weights still dominate HBM bandwidth at every point on the curve, so decode stays weight-bound rather than tipping into a KV-bound regime. A useful reference point for anyone in the @NVIDIA AI community evaluating consumer Ampere for dual-GPU inference.

Prefill converges upward toward ~1,170 tok/s as prompts get large enough to amortize fixed per-request overhead. TTFT for a 10K-token conversation lands around 9 seconds. For async workloads that's fine; for interactive chat it's the dimension users will notice.

---

**Turn off thinking mode if you don't need it**

Gemma 4 has thinking enabled by default. Left on, a typical response burns ~200 output tokens on internal reasoning before producing anything visible — that's roughly 8 seconds of "nothing happening" on my setup. I patched the chat template to disable it. Response time dropped from 8.3s to 2.3s with identical generation speed. The savings come entirely from not generating tokens you don't want.

If you're replacing a hosted API with a local deployment and your workload doesn't need the chain-of-thought, this is a free 3.6x latency improvement.

---

**Why llama.cpp and not vLLM?**

I tried vLLM first. Its FP8 weight-only path produced ~16.5GB per GPU — about 1GB more than llama.cpp's Q8_0, and on a 24GB card, 1GB is the difference between fitting and OOMing. llama.cpp's lower overhead comes from memory-mapping the embedding table to CPU and not maintaining NCCL buffers, CUDA graph pools, or sampler warmup state. For tight-VRAM consumer deployments, that lower overhead is a feature, not a limitation. vLLM's additional memory is the price of continuous batching, PagedAttention, and production-grade concurrent serving — features that matter at scale but don't help a single-user deployment that can't even start.

---

**The consumer build gotcha that cost 21%**

This is the finding I'd least expect most people to know before they buy hardware.

I reran the same benchmark with the GPU pair swapped from `0,2` (NVLink) to `0,1` (no NVLink, PCIe fallback). At 28K tokens, prefill dropped from 994 tok/s to 911 tok/s — a 21% hit. At mid-range prompts the gap peaked at 30%. Decode was unaffected.

The naive math said the hit should be about 1.5%. Pipeline parallelism only transfers activations once per forward pass, and the payload is small. So why was it so much worse?

Because on my motherboard, GPUs 0 and 1 can't do peer-to-peer DMA. `nvidia-smi topo -p2p r` reports `CNS` (chipset not supported) for every non-NVLink pair. When peer access is denied, the CUDA runtime silently falls back to staging through pinned host memory: GPU 0 → system RAM → GPU 1. That doubles the total PCIe traffic, serializes with host memory operations, and adds stream synchronization overhead on every transfer.

The lesson: on a consumer motherboard where additional GPU slots hang off the chipset rather than the CPU's direct PCIe lanes, multi-GPU inference pays a host-staging cost that can be substantially worse than the link's nominal bandwidth would suggest. Two boards with identical spec sheets — same slot count, same lane widths — can behave very differently depending on which slots share a root complex with the CPU.

---

**What to check before you buy**

If you're building a consumer multi-GPU inference rig, run this on any system you're considering (or ask someone who has one to):

```
nvidia-smi topo -p2p r
```

`OK` between your intended GPU pair means peer-to-peer works. `CNS` means you'll be paying the host-staging penalty on every cross-GPU transfer. That one command tells you more about the practical multi-GPU performance of a consumer motherboard than any spec sheet will.
