# Week 8, Day 3 — Gemma 4 26B-A4B MoE: Head-to-Head with the Dense Sibling

**Training Program:** RTX 3090 AI Infrastructure — Phase 2: Production Inference at Scale
**Date:** April 5, 2026
**Hardware:** 4x RTX 3090 (48GB NVLink pair: GPU 0+2), Ubuntu 24.04, CUDA 12.6
**Model:** Gemma 4 31B Dense IT (Q8_0 GGUF via llama.cpp)

## Summary

Deployed Gemma 4 26B-A4B MoE on the NVLink-bridged RTX 3090 pair via llama.cpp and ran the same throughput sweep used for the 31B Dense model on Day 2, plus the PCIe-topology variant. The MoE wins by 4.7x on decode and 3.6x on prefill at long context, and fits Gemma 4's full 262K context window where the dense model was forced to auto-shrink to 104K. A new finding emerged on the PCIe run that was not visible on the dense model: MoE decode shows a consistent ~6% PCIe penalty across every context length, where dense decode was noise-level unaffected. The mechanism is that MoE's faster per-token compute finally makes fixed PCIe handshake overhead visible as a fraction of the per-token budget.

## What Happened

**Model load and architectural inspection.** Downloaded `gemma-4-26B-A4B-it-Q8_0.gguf` and launched llama-server with the same parameters used for the dense model on Day 2, changing only the model file. The startup log confirmed a clean structural relationship to the dense variant: 30 layers vs 60, hidden dim 2816 vs 5376, 16 attention heads vs 32, 8 / 2 KV heads on SWA / global layers vs 16 / 4. The MoE is essentially a half-depth, half-width version of the dense architecture with MoE FFN blocks (128 experts, 8 routed per token, expert FFN dim 704) swapped in for the dense FFN path. 25 of 30 layers use sliding-window attention with a 1024-token window; 5 are global. That 25/5 ratio mirrors the 50/10 ratio of the dense model exactly.

**Memory observation.** llama.cpp's `-fit` mode auto-sized context for both models. The dense model had to shrink from Gemma 4's native 262K window down to 104K to fit VRAM; the MoE loaded the full 262K with 11.4 GB of free VRAM remaining. Total VRAM used at maximum context: 45.7 GB dense at 104K, 36.3 GB MoE at 262K. The mechanism driving this is KV cache scaling: MoE has half the layers (30 vs 60), and its global attention layers use 2 KV heads instead of 4. Per-cell KV cost on the MoE is therefore roughly a quarter of the dense model's, which is why 2.5x more context cells fit in half the KV cache memory. This was a bigger memory win than expected at the outset — I was thinking of it as "slightly more efficient" and it turned out to be a categorical difference in what you can actually load.

**NVLink throughput sweep.** Ran `exp2_throughput_sweep.py` (updated today with a `--model-name` parameter so the output JSON no longer collides with the dense results) against the MoE with `CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES=0,2`. Single-stream decode landed at 112 tok/s at short context and 94 tok/s at 28K. Prefill reached 4,276 tok/s at 14K tokens and held ~4,200 tok/s at 28K. The ratios against the dense model were strikingly clean: decode was a flat 4.6–4.7x multiplier at every context length; prefill was 2.6x at short context and grew to 3.6x at long context.

**Wrong prediction caught and corrected during analysis.** Before the PCIe sweep I worked through an activation-payload byte count: at 28K tokens, the dense model moves ~301 MB across the pipeline boundary per forward pass (hidden dim 5376 × 28000 × 2 bytes); the MoE moves ~158 MB (hidden dim 2816). I then argued that because MoE compute shrinks faster than transfer bytes, the ratio of transfer time to compute time would grow on the MoE, and predicted a 30–45% PCIe prefill penalty at 28K compared to dense's measured 21%. The actual measurement came in at 24% — directionally right (slightly larger than dense) but overshooting badly in magnitude. The framing was correct that transfer-to-compute ratio matters; what I missed was that llama.cpp's prefill transfer likely overlaps with compute well enough that absolute transfer bytes dominate the penalty more than the ratio argument suggested. Worth remembering: "compute shrank more than transfer did" is a real effect but not a proportional one, and simple first-order arguments about ratio shifts can overstate the consequences.

**PCIe sweep and the new decode-penalty finding.** Re-ran the sweep with `CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES=0,1`, which forces activation transfer across the PCIe 3.0 x1 slot on the consumer motherboard instead of the NVLink bridge. Prefill took a 14–30% hit across the curve, peaking at 29.6% at 3.5K tokens and settling to 24% at 28K — in the same ballpark as dense's 21% at long context. Decode, however, showed a consistent ~6% penalty across every context length, where the dense model's decode had been noise-level unaffected by the same topology change. The mechanism is that MoE's per-token decode budget is ~10 ms vs ~48 ms for dense, and fixed PCIe handshake overhead (latency, CUDA launch cost, kernel sync — not raw bandwidth) takes a bigger bite out of a shorter budget. The activation payload itself is trivial either way (~5.6 KB per token on MoE), so this is not a bytes-over-wire story; it's a fixed-overhead-per-transfer story finally becoming visible. The broader principle is that accelerating compute exposes communication costs that were previously hidden, even on per-token workloads that seemed bandwidth-trivial.

**GPU pair slip caught early.** Started the MoE server with `CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES` still set from a previous session, realized on the first nvidia-smi check that the model had loaded on GPUs 0 and 1 (the PCIe pair) instead of 0 and 2 (the NVLink pair). Restarted with the correct pair and re-verified before running the NVLink sweep. This would have been hours of confused debugging if the sweep had run first and produced "NVLink" numbers that were actually PCIe numbers.

**Script updated for reproducibility.** Added a `--model-name` argument to `exp2_throughput_sweep.py`. The name now flows through to the request payload's `model` field, the summary table header, the JSON metadata, and the default output filename (`results/exp2_throughput_sweep_<model-name>.json`). Removed the previously hardcoded `context_limit: 104704` from the JSON metadata since it was specific to the dense run and would have silently misrepresented the MoE's 262K context in the archived results.

**LinkedIn Pulse article drafted.** Full head-to-head write-up with throughput and memory tables, architectural framing, the PCIe topology replication, and a deliberately-hedged section on multi-user deployment implications (memory headroom and per-token speed suggest viability for small-team deployment; no concurrent-load benchmark was run, so no measured multi-user claims). Targeted for Tuesday night publication to give yesterday's dense-variant post two business days to land first.

## Key Learnings

1. **MoE decode speedup is a flat multiplier across context length.** The 4.6–4.7x advantage was constant from ~520 to ~28,085 prompt tokens. This is the expected shape when the speedup comes from a pure weight-bandwidth mechanism that doesn't interact with sequence length. The measured 4.7x vs a theoretical 7.7x ceiling (active params 4B / total dense 30.7B) gives ~61% realized efficiency; the dilution comes from per-token costs that MoE doesn't reduce — KV cache traffic, attention compute on the small number of global layers, router overhead.

2. **MoE prefill discount applies at all batch sizes, not just small ones.** My initial intuition was that at prefill batch sizes of thousands of tokens, enough tokens would collectively route to enough experts to approximate activating the full expert bank and erase the compute savings. This was wrong. Each token independently routes to only 8 of 128 experts regardless of batch size; the compute budget is `tokens × active_experts × expert_size`, and the MoE discount is preserved. This matters for any future reasoning about MoE prefill behavior.

3. **Hybrid attention keeps prefill FFN-bound even at long context.** With 25 of 30 layers using 1024-token sliding window attention, the prefill compute curve stays nearly linear in context length instead of going O(n²). That's why the MoE prefill advantage *grows* with context (2.6x → 3.6x) rather than shrinking — FFN compute keeps dominating, so the FFN discount keeps mattering. On a hypothetical architecture with fully global attention, the MoE advantage would be expected to shrink at long context as attention started dominating total FLOPs.

4. **Per-cell KV cost is the memory lever, not total parameter count.** The MoE fits 2.5x more context cells in half the KV memory because each cell costs roughly a quarter. Half comes from layer count (30 vs 60), and the remaining half comes from halved KV head counts on the global layers specifically. When sizing local deployments for long context, "how much KV per token per layer" is the quantity to optimize, and it's largely decoupled from total parameter count.

5. **MoE exposes PCIe decode latency that dense hid.** New finding from today's PCIe sweep: MoE decode shows a consistent ~6% PCIe penalty where dense decode was noise-level unaffected. The activation payload is trivial either way; the mechanism is fixed per-transfer overhead (latency, kernel launch, sync) being a larger fraction of a shorter per-token budget. General principle: accelerating compute exposes communication costs that were previously hidden. This will matter more, not less, as future models push active parameter counts down while keeping total parameters high.

6. **"Deployment category" is a more useful frame than raw throughput.** The 31B Dense and 26B-A4B MoE look similar on a spec sheet and are both in the "large local model" conceptual bucket. In practice, on this hardware, they belong to different deployment categories: the dense model is a single-user desktop deployment with cramped context; the MoE is a plausible small-team shared inference target with the full context window and substantial VRAM headroom. For future framework and model evaluation, asking "what deployment category does this put me in?" is more actionable than asking "how fast is it?"

7. **llama.cpp layer-split parallelism has exactly one boundary crossing per forward pass.** Corrected an earlier misconception that layer depth would affect the number of pipeline-stage handoffs. Under `--split-mode layer` (the default), layers are assigned contiguously — first N on GPU 0, remaining on GPU 1 — so there is one activation transfer per forward pass regardless of whether the model has 30 or 60 layers. What scales with architecture is the size of the activation payload at that single crossing, not the number of crossings.

8. **Predict-before-measure discipline is worth the effort even when the prediction is wrong.** The activation-payload math predicted a 30–45% MoE PCIe prefill penalty; actual was 24%. The prediction was wrong in magnitude, but the process forced an explicit articulation of the mechanism, which made it easier to see *why* the prediction was off when the measurement came in. The debugged version of the reasoning is more durable than either "I was right" or an unexamined measurement would have been.

9. **Prefix caching changes the fine-tuning calculus for mostly-static prompts.** The efficiency argument for fine-tuning a system prompt away assumes per-request prefill costs. With prefix caching (on by default in llama.cpp), those costs collapse to once-per-session for the static portion of the prompt, reducing the real savings from prompt elimination to near-zero for typical conversational usage. This makes in-context prompting the architecturally correct choice for reference-documentation-heavy prompts regardless of their length, provided the caching layer is in place and the deployment pattern allows sessions to reuse it.

## Implications for statmon-ai

Today's MoE results, combined with an honest reread of the statmon-ai system prompt, prompted a significant shift in the planned approach for the application. The original plan had been to fine-tune the 31B Dense model on recently-generated training data, with the goal of eliminating (or drastically shrinking) the ~5K token system prompt. Two findings from today force a rethink.

**First, the efficiency argument for removing the system prompt has collapsed.** The MoE's 3.6x prefill advantage at long context reduces the ~5K prompt's prefill cost from ~5 seconds (dense) to ~1.25 seconds (MoE). Combined with llama.cpp's default prefix caching — which yesterday's cache-reuse debugging confirmed is active and effective unless explicitly defeated — that ~1.25-second cost is paid once per session rather than per request. For a typical statmon-ai investigation session with multiple back-and-forth queries, the amortized per-request cost of the static prompt prefix approaches zero. The original plan assumed per-request prefill costs that don't exist in a properly-caching deployment.

**Second, an audit of the statmon-ai system prompt reveals a composition that's structurally mismatched with fine-tuning.** Roughly 75% of the prompt is reference documentation — command names, enum values, filter syntax, attribute lists, valid flag values for the Statmon and auth-querystore CLIs. Another ~10% is tool descriptions (whois, dns_resolve, etc.). Only ~20% is behavioral content (investigation playbooks) and ~5% is role framing and behavioral guidelines. Fine-tuning is a poor tool for baking reference documentation into weights: models hallucinate exact enum values, substitute plausible-looking but incorrect command names (hyphens vs underscores, singular vs plural), and drift out of sync with the underlying system whenever the real CLI changes. Reference material is exactly what in-context prompting handles verbatim and auditably; fine-tuning is exactly where it handles it worst. The `{nodes_section}` template placeholder already in the prompt is a signal that the content is meant to be parameterized and updated at runtime, which is fundamentally incompatible with baking it into weights.

At best, fine-tuning could target the ~20% investigation-patterns portion of the prompt — procedural playbooks like "for a SERVFAIL drill-down, run these commands in this order" — where behavioral-pattern baking is what fine-tuning is actually good at. Realistic token savings: ~800–1,000 tokens, meaningful but not transformative, and tied to a specific base model checkpoint that would need retraining whenever the underlying model is swapped.

**Revised approach for statmon-ai:** Deploy the 26B-A4B MoE in production with the full existing system prompt and prefix caching enabled. This gives better quality (no hallucinated CLI commands), better maintainability (edit a string to update), and per-session latency that's already in acceptable territory for interactive operational use. The efficiency concerns that originally motivated fine-tuning are addressed structurally by MoE compute characteristics and the caching layer, not by baking content into weights.

**Revised approach for the fine-tuning learning goal:** Decouple it entirely from statmon-ai. Fine-tuning remains a valuable skill to develop through direct experimentation, but the right vehicle is a smaller model (likely 3B–7B) on a cleaner task where fine-tuning's strengths — output formatting constraints, domain-specific reasoning patterns, consistent tool-call shapes, behavioral defaults — actually apply. QLoRA on a single 3090 gives fast iteration loops and mature tooling, and the skills transfer upward to larger models when the right application comes along. The specific model and task are decisions for when this experiment is scheduled, not now.

**Open question deferred to a future day:** statmon-ai currently represents a narrow slice of a broader operational scope. Extending the tool surface — additional CacheServe commands once they're implemented, other operational tooling, additional investigation domains — will grow the system prompt proportionally. At some threshold, prefix caching alone stops being sufficient and architectural approaches become relevant: tool-based retrieval of CLI syntax (a `describe_command` tool the model calls when it needs reference lookup), router-based prompt selection (load the SecOps section only when investigating security issues), or RAG over a reference corpus that can scale independently of the context window. None of this is relevant at the current ~5K prompt size, but it's the class of problem that will matter before the prompt approaches 15–20K tokens. Flagging it here so it's not forgotten when the scope expansion eventually happens.
